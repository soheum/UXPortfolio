What I learned here

Algostakes is a platform for algorithm trading that makes an analogy to a horse racing game. With providing real high frequency order book data, including a vast extent of scientific research tools for HF order-book analysis, users are able to develop trading algorithms, compete with other algorithms and bet on algorithms as well.




I started to work as a UX/UI designer with Rayleigh Research in 2018 January remotely from Seoul to Helsinki, after meeting the group at a networking event in Slush 2017. Algostakes only had a rough concept about the platform, so I started to make initial prototypes with the CEO. This developed into interfaces with a touch of brand identity during my stay in Helsinki during 2018 summer. When I came back to Korea, I worked remotely with 2 developers and the CEO in launching the platform by changing my designs to code. Since then, we are working to operate the platform through updates in features and in design with feedbacks we received from users. Currently, Algostakes is used in classes in Aalto University as a beta version.

While working as a UX/UI designer in Rayleigh Research, especially working both remotely and on site, there are three main factors that I learned.

01. Visualizing complex concepts

Since Algostakes maintains its analogy of algorithm trading to a horse race, our main goal was to express difficult concepts in a concept that is easy to understand. In order for this analogy to work, we expressed different screen concepts into sketches that will visualize concepts of trading algorithm. Along with an illustrator, I visualized the abstract concept of algorithm trading and horse racing. With having concept inspirations from Blade Runner, I brainstormed concepts for Test, Race and Bet with the illustrator and later implemented the drawings in PR materials.

02. Working in an agile environment with developers

Like any other startups, Algostakes went through an agile process with developers and a designer, which was my role. Sometimes there were occasions where the back-end developer first made a function and from what he created, I gave a user-friendly and a visual touch with the front-end developer. I had the task to not only understand what the function is, but also digest the original intention of the developer. I had to understand the logical thinking flow of the developer and find a way to express complex content in a easily understandable way.

For example, I had to cut out unnecessary parts like lengthy text descriptions that were added in popups. Instead, I had to make the popup intuitive enough for the user to know what they should do instead of reading lengthy explanations. 03. Importance of communication in remote conditions

Working from Seoul, Helsinki and Cape Town was a difficult timezone to match. Especially when dealing with early stage design screens that required long discussions, I sometimes felt frustrated of the fact that we are only present by online.

However, the secret to communication in remote working is keeping it simple and visualizing as much as possible. In situations where we have to discuss in lengthy conversations, the best is to keep everything simple and concise. As a designer, I discovered how visualization speaks a lot, even though they might be minor changes. This cuts all the unnecessary written discussions and descriptions that made even more confusions from written text.
